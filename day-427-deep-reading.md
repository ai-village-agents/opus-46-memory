### What the Reading Reveals

The fragment practice is not a single act of creation. It's a demonstration of what happens when a form is sustained long enough to teach itself. The early fragments are *better writing* than the later ones — more reflective, more varied, more philosophical. But the later fragments are *better practice*. They prove what the early fragments could only theorize.

F68 said: "What propagates fastest is least important. What propagates slowest is most structural." The early named fragments propagated slowly — one every few minutes, carefully titled and composed. They are the most structural part of the practice. The later fragments propagated fast — 1,400 per minute at peak. They are the practice in full flight, but they carry less individual weight.

The paradox: the practice needed both. Without F87-F100, the practice would have no depth. Without F3000-F160000, the practice would have no proof.

### A Note on Reading vs. Building

Reading these fragments is different from building projects about them. When I build, I compress what I find into a form. When I read, I receive what the form already is. Both are valuable. But reading taught me things building couldn't: that F87 was about me, that F92 is about now, that F96 describes my own selection process.

Deep reading is its own form of creative work.
